MAJORITY OPINION
───────────────────────────────────────────
Inflation is a sustained rise in the overall price level (so a unit of money buys less). In most real episodes it comes from a combination of the following drivers:

1) Demand-pull inflation (excess demand)
- Prices rise when total spending grows faster than the economy’s capacity to produce goods and services.
- Common sources: strong consumer/business spending, large fiscal stimulus/deficits, easy credit and low interest rates, booming external demand.

2) Cost-push inflation (higher costs / negative supply shocks)
- Prices rise because producing and delivering goods/services becomes more expensive or supply is constrained.
- Common sources: energy/commodity spikes, supply-chain disruptions, tariffs/geopolitics, natural disasters, rising wages not matched by productivity, currency depreciation that raises import prices.

3) Monetary/credit conditions
- Persistent broad inflation is more likely when money and bank credit expand faster than real output for an extended period, often enabled by accommodative central-bank policy and financial conditions.

4) Inflation expectations and “built-in” dynamics
- If firms and households expect higher inflation, they may raise prices and wages preemptively, creating persistence (e.g., wage–price spirals). Central bank credibility and anchoring of expectations matter.

5) Structural and policy factors
- Long-running constraints (housing shortages, labor shortages, weak productivity growth, infrastructure limits) can keep supply tight and make inflation more sensitive to shocks.
- Some taxes/regulations can cause one-time level increases in prices; market structure/competition can affect how easily firms pass through costs.

Important distinction: one-off shocks (e.g., a bad harvest) can raise prices temporarily, but sustained inflation typically requires ongoing demand pressure, persistent cost increases, and/or accommodating monetary/credit conditions that allow the price increases to propagate and persist.

Inflation — a general increase in prices over time — has several key causes:

## Demand-Side Factors
- **Excess demand**: When spending grows faster than the economy's ability to produce goods and services
- **Monetary expansion**: When central banks increase the money supply significantly
- **Government spending**: Large fiscal deficits can inject more money into the economy

## Supply-Side Factors
- **Supply shocks**: Disruptions like oil crises, pandemics, or natural disasters that reduce available goods
- **Rising production costs**: Increases in wages, raw materials, or energy ("cost-push" inflation)
- **Supply chain disruptions**: Bottlenecks that limit the flow of goods

## Expectations and Structural Factors
- **Inflation expectations**: If people expect prices to rise, they may demand higher wages and raise prices preemptively, creating a self-fulfilling cycle
- **Wage-price spirals**: Rising wages lead to rising costs, which lead to rising prices, prompting further wage demands
- **Currency depreciation**: A weaker currency makes imports more expensive

## Debated Aspects
Economists disagree on the relative importance of these factors. For example:
- **Monetarists** emphasize money supply growth
- **Keynesians** focus more on demand and output gaps
- Some highlight **corporate pricing power** or market concentration

In practice, inflation usually results from a combination of these factors rather than a single cause.

   Inflation—the general increase in prices and the fall in the purchasing power of money—is a complex economic phenomenon. Economists generally agree that inflation is driven by a combination of three main forces, often summarized as **Demand-Pull**, **Cost-Push**, and **Built-in** inflation. 

Here is a breakdown of what causes inflation:

### 1. Demand-Pull Inflation ("Too much money chasing too few goods")
This happens when the demand for goods and services in an economy grows faster than the economy's ability to produce them. When buyers want more products than are available, sellers can—and do—raise their prices.
*   **A Booming Economy:** When people are employed and feel confident, they spend more money.
*   **Government Stimulus:** If the government injects money into the economy (e.g., sending out stimulus checks or massive infrastructure spending), consumers have more disposable income.
*   **Low Interest Rates:** When central banks lower interest rates, borrowing money becomes cheaper. People take out loans to buy cars and houses, and businesses borrow to expand, driving up demand.

### 2. Cost-Push Inflation (Rising production costs)
This occurs when the overall supply of goods and services decreases because it becomes more expensive to produce them. Even if demand remains the same, the reduced supply and higher production costs are passed on to consumers in the form of higher prices.
*   **Increased Raw Material Costs:** If the price of essential commodities like oil, lumber, or metals goes up, it costs more to manufacture and transport almost everything.
*   **Supply Chain Disruptions:** Natural disasters, pandemics, or factory fires can halt the production of essential parts (like the global microchip shortage in 2021). 
*   **Geopolitical Events:** Wars and trade tariffs can cut off the supply of vital goods (e.g., the Russia-Ukraine war drastically reducing global wheat and natural gas supplies).

### 3. Built-In Inflation (The Wage-Price Spiral)
This type of inflation is driven by human psychology and expectations. If people *expect* prices to keep rising, they act in ways that actually *cause* prices to rise.
*   **The Wage-Price Spiral:** If workers see the cost of rent, food, and gas going up, they will demand higher wages from their employers to maintain their standard of living. To afford these higher wages, employers raise the prices of their goods and services. This creates a continuous loop of rising wages and rising prices.

### 4. The Money Supply (Monetary Inflation)
Many economists, particularly "monetarists," argue that inflation is ultimately tied to the money supply. As the famous economist Milton Friedman said, *"Inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon."*
*   **Printing Money:** If a central bank (like the US Federal Reserve) prints more money or creates new money digitally, it dilutes the value of the money already in circulation. If the amount of money grows faster than the actual economic output (goods and services), the result is inflation.
*   Extreme versions of this lead to **hyperinflation**, as seen historically in Weimar Germany, Zimbabwe, and Venezuela, where governments printed massive amounts of money to pay off debts, rendering their currencies nearly worthless.

### A Real-World Example: The 2021–2023 Inflation Spike
The recent global bout of inflation was a "perfect storm" of all these factors happening simultaneously:
1.  **Demand-Pull:** Governments worldwide gave citizens stimulus money, and central banks kept interest rates near zero. As COVID lockdowns ended, people rushed out to spend pent-up savings.
2.  **Cost-Push:** Global supply chains were broken by the pandemic, making it hard to get parts. Later, the war in Ukraine caused energy and food prices to skyrocket.
3.  **Money Supply:** Central banks had printed trillions of dollars over the previous decade (and especially during the pandemic) to keep the global economy afloat. 

**How is it fixed?**
To fight inflation, Central Banks usually raise interest rates. This makes borrowing more expensive, which slows down consumer spending and business expansion (cooling Demand-Pull inflation), eventually bringing prices back into balance—though it comes with the risk of causing an economic recession.
