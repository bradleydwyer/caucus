Inflation (a sustained rise in the overall price level) is caused when **total nominal spending in the economy grows faster than the economy’s capacity to produce real goods and services**—and it *persists* when policies and expectations allow that imbalance to continue.

That broad condition can be generated through a few main channels:

1) **Demand-pull inflation (excess demand)**
- Households, firms, and government collectively try to buy more than the economy can supply at current prices.
- Drivers: strong job/wage growth, credit booms, large fiscal stimulus, very low interest rates, wealth effects that boost spending.

2) **Cost-push / supply-shock inflation (reduced supply or higher inputs)**
- Production/distribution becomes harder or more expensive, so costs rise and/or shortages let sellers raise prices.
- Examples: oil/energy spikes, food shocks, supply-chain disruptions, tariffs, natural disasters, pandemics, wars.
- **Currency depreciation** is a common mechanism here because it makes imports more expensive.

3) **Monetary and financial conditions (what makes it sustained and broad-based)**
- A one-time shock can raise the *price level* temporarily, but **ongoing inflation** typically requires that **money/credit conditions and interest-rate policy accommodate continued growth in nominal spending** beyond real output growth.
- This is the precise version of “too much money chasing too few goods”: not every price jump is “money printing,” but persistent economy-wide inflation is hard to maintain without supportive monetary/financial conditions.

4) **Expectations and wage–price dynamics (persistence)**
- If workers and firms expect higher inflation, they build it into wage demands, contracts, and pricing, which can become self-reinforcing.
- Central-bank credibility matters because it helps keep expectations anchored.

5) **Sectoral/structural constraints (composition and propagation)**
- Areas with slow supply response—especially **housing/rents**—can keep measured inflation elevated for long periods and spill over into other prices.

**On “greedflation”:** profit-seeking and market power can affect *how much* and *how fast* prices adjust (an amplifier/transmission mechanism), but by itself it doesn’t usually explain **sustained, economy-wide** inflation without an underlying demand/supply imbalance and accommodating macro conditions.

Inflation—a general rise in prices over time—has several commonly identified causes:

## Demand-Side Factors
- **Excess demand:** When spending (by consumers, businesses, or government) grows faster than the economy's ability to produce goods and services
- **Monetary expansion:** When the money supply grows significantly, more money can chase the same amount of goods
- **Low interest rates:** Cheap borrowing encourages spending and investment

## Supply-Side Factors
- **Supply shocks:** Disruptions like oil crises, natural disasters, or pandemics that reduce available goods
- **Rising production costs:** Increases in wages, raw materials, or energy costs that businesses pass on to consumers
- **Supply chain disruptions:** Bottlenecks that limit the flow of goods

## Other Contributing Factors
- **Inflation expectations:** If people *expect* prices to rise, they may demand higher wages and raise prices preemptively, creating a self-fulfilling cycle
- **Government fiscal policy:** Large budget deficits financed by borrowing or money creation
- **Currency depreciation:** A weaker currency makes imports more expensive
- **Structural factors:** Reduced competition or monopoly power allowing firms to raise prices

## Worth Noting
Economists debate the relative importance of these factors. Monetarists emphasize money supply growth, Keynesians focus on demand and output gaps, and others highlight supply-side or institutional factors. In practice, inflation often results from a combination of causes.

3. gemini-3.1-pro-preview wrote:
   At its most basic level, inflation is defined by the classic economic phrase: **"Too much money chasing too few goods."** When this happens, the purchasing power of your money decreases, meaning a dollar buys less today than it did yesterday. 

Economists generally divide the causes of inflation into three main categories, alongside a few underlying drivers.

---

### 1. Demand-Pull Inflation
This is the most common cause of inflation. It happens when the demand for goods and services outpaces the economy's ability to produce them. Because consumers are competing to buy limited products, businesses can raise their prices.
* **Economic Booms:** When unemployment is low and wages are high, people have more disposable income to spend.
* **Government Stimulus:** If the government sends out stimulus checks or cuts taxes, consumers suddenly have more money to spend.
* **Low Interest Rates:** When it is cheap to borrow money, people and businesses take out loans to buy cars, homes, and equipment, driving up demand.

### 2. Cost-Push Inflation
This occurs when the cost of producing goods and services increases, and businesses pass those higher costs on to consumers in the form of higher prices. In this scenario, demand hasn't changed, but the supply has become more expensive.
* **Raw Material Shocks:** If the price of oil skyrockets, it costs more to manufacture plastics, fuel airplanes, and transport goods. The price of almost everything goes up as a result.
* **Labor Costs:** If a shortage of workers forces companies to raise wages drastically to attract employees, the companies will raise the prices of their products to cover the higher payroll.
* **Natural Disasters or War:** Events that destroy crops, damage factories, or disrupt global trade (like the war in Ukraine affecting global grain and energy supplies) reduce supply and drive up costs.

### 3. Built-In Inflation (The Wage-Price Spiral)
This type of inflation is driven by human psychology and expectations. 
* If workers expect prices to keep rising, they will demand higher wages from their employers to maintain their standard of living.
* To afford these higher wages, employers raise the prices of their goods and services.
* Workers see prices rising again and demand even higher wages, creating a continuous loop known as the **wage-price spiral**.

---

### The Underlying Drivers of Inflation

While the three points above explain *how* prices rise, the following explain *why* those conditions exist in the first place:

**Expansion of the Money Supply**
Famed economist Milton Friedman once said, "Inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon." If a central bank (like the U.S. Federal Reserve) prints more money or injects too much credit into the banking system, the currency loses its value. If the money supply grows faster than the economic output, prices will inevitably rise. Extreme versions of this lead to *hyperinflation*, as seen historically in Zimbabwe, Venezuela, and Weimar Germany.

**Supply Chain Disruptions**
In an interconnected global economy, a bottleneck in one part of the world can cause inflation everywhere else. For example, during the COVID-19 pandemic, computer chip factories shut down. This meant automakers couldn't finish building new cars. Because new cars were scarce, the price of both new and used cars skyrocketed. 

**Corporate Pricing Power ("Greedflation")**
A more highly debated cause in modern economics is when large corporations use periods of broad inflation or supply shocks as an "excuse" to raise their prices higher than necessary, expanding their profit margins simply because consumers have already accepted that prices are going up. 

### Summary
Inflation is rarely caused by just one thing. The severe inflation the world experienced in 2021 and 2022, for example, was a "perfect storm": Governments pumped trillions of dollars into the economy to prevent a COVID depression (**demand-pull**), supply chains broke down so goods were scarce (**cost-push**), and the war in Ukraine caused energy and food prices to spike (**cost-push**).
