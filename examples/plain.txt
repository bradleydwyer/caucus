Inflation is a sustained rise in the overall price level (money buys less). It typically arises when economy-wide spending grows faster than the economy’s capacity to produce, when production costs rise broadly, or when inflation becomes embedded in expectations.

Key causes/mechanisms:
- Demand-pull inflation: Aggregate demand outstrips supply (e.g., strong consumer/business spending, fiscal stimulus, easy credit/low interest rates, export booms).
- Cost-push / supply shocks: Firms face higher input costs or reduced supply and raise prices (e.g., energy/food spikes, supply-chain disruptions, wars, disasters, pandemics, labor shortages). Currency depreciation can amplify this by raising import prices.
- Monetary/credit conditions: Persistent growth of money and credit faster than real output tends to sustain inflation over time; central bank policy (rates, asset purchases, financial conditions) influences this and inflation persistence.
- Expectations and wage–price dynamics: If households/firms expect higher inflation, workers seek higher wages and firms set higher prices, creating self-reinforcing persistence (a wage–price spiral), especially in tight labor markets.
- Structural/policy factors: Sector bottlenecks and institutions (e.g., housing shortages, tariffs, reduced competition/market concentration, regulation) can keep prices elevated in specific areas and sometimes spill over.

In practice, inflation episodes are usually a mix (e.g., strong demand + supply constraints + shifting expectations). Persistence depends heavily on expectations/wage-setting and whether monetary policy accommodates or restrains the shock.
